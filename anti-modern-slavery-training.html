Lesson 1

What is Modern Slavery?

Definition

Modern slavery, while having no universal legal definition, is an umbrella term used to describe a range of different exploitative crimes where a person cannot refuse or leave the situation because of threats, violence, coercion, deception, or abuse of power. Simply put, it is the exploitation of another person for personal or commercial gain.

Modern slavery sits at the extreme of a continuum of decent work and severe exploitation. Although practices of substandard working conditions or underpayment are illegal, these issues can escalate into situations of modern slavery.

The Scale of the Problem

Despite the abolition movement of the past and current international treaty obligations, slavery is thriving now more than at any other time in history. There are more people trapped in slavery today than at any other point in time.

In 2023, Walk Free and the International Labour Organization (ILO), together with the International Organization for Migration (IOM), published the Global Estimates of Modern Slavery — the authoritative report that also forms the basis of the 2023 Global Slavery Index. As of 2021, it was estimated that over 50 million people worldwide were living in modern slavery on any given day — an increase of approximately 10 million since 2016.

Forced labour occurs across sectors including domestic work, construction, agriculture, fishing, manufacturing, and services. The majority of forced labor occurs in the private economy, rather than being imposed directly by the state. Forced marriage disproportionately affects women and girls.

Over 12 million children are living in situations of modern slavery. It is important to note that child labor is a broader category — modern slavery represents the most severe and coercive forms of exploitation within it.

Lesson 2

Types of Modern Slavery

Legal Definitions

Taken from the 1956 UN Supplementary Convention on the Abolition of Slavery, definitions of modern-day slavery commonly include: "debt bondage, serfdom, forced marriage and the delivery of a child for the exploitation of that child are all slavery-like practices and require criminalization and abolishment."

The 1930 Forced Labour Convention defines forced labor as: "all work or service which is exacted from any person under the menace of any penalty and for which the said person has not offered himself voluntarily."

The 8 Types of Modern Slavery

1
Servitude

A condition where a person is forced to work against their will under threats, coercion, or deception, with restrictions on their freedom of movement. This can include domestic servitude, forced labor in agriculture or mining, and bonded labor where people are forced to work to pay off debts.

2
Forced Marriage

A marriage where one or both parties are forced to marry through coercion, threats, or deception. This can be used as a form of control, exploitation, or to gain access to resources or political power.

3
Human Trafficking

The movement of people through force, fraud, or coercion for exploitation, including forced labor, sexual exploitation, organ removal, or domestic servitude. It can occur within or across borders and involves deception, threats, or abuse of power.

4
Forced Labor

Work or service extracted from a person through threats, violence, intimidation, or coercion, or by abuse of vulnerability. This can include physical or psychological force, debt bondage, or threats of deportation.

5
Slavery and Slave-like Practices

The state of ownership of another human being, treating them as property, and exploiting their labor or services without their consent or compensation. This includes traditional forms of chattel slavery, modern slavery practices, and practices similar to slavery, such as debt bondage, serfdom, and forced marriage.

6
Child Labor

Any work or service that is harmful to children's physical, mental, social, or moral development, exploitative, or interferes with their schooling. This includes hazardous work, forced labor, trafficking, sexual exploitation, and armed conflict.

7
Deceptive Recruitment

False or misleading information or promises used to lure people into situations of forced labor or trafficking. This can involve promises of high wages, better working conditions, or educational opportunities, leading them into exploitation.

8
Debt Bondage

A system where a person is forced to work to pay off a debt, often inherited or incurred under false pretenses, with no control over the repayment terms or conditions. This can trap people in cycles of exploitation and prevent them from leaving their situation.

Note: These are definitions, and the realities of modern slavery can be complex and multifaceted. It's important to understand the nuances and different forms these practices can take to effectively identify and combat them.

Lesson 3

Spot the Signs

The indicators below are the most common potential signs of modern slavery. While the presence of one or more of these markers may not necessarily mean that the person is being exploited, it could suggest that they are a potential victim. Consider each situation individually.

If in doubt, always say something to the appropriate reporting bodies and approach these conversations with discretion and sensitivity.

Victims of modern slavery may be hesitant or afraid to tell their story through fear of reprisal or not being believed. They might also feel a sense of shame or be unaware of their rights and the treatment they are entitled to receive.

Signs of Modern Slavery

Click each tile to learn more.

👁 Controlled by Another Person +
They are accompanied by a controlling person and do not speak on their own behalf, instead deferring to another person. They cannot move freely and have a lack of discretion or choice over personal life decisions. They may be unable to terminate employment freely or leave their situation at any time. They may not know their work or home address. They are forced to stay in accommodation provided by their employer, which may be overcrowded.
💸 Lack of Earnings +
They cannot keep their earnings, or another person tells them it is held for "safekeeping." As a result, they never have any money on them. They may be afraid to accept money or payment for something. An exploiter may take their legitimate wages. In many cases, they owe a debt they are working to pay off.
😔 Overly Fearful, Depressed, or Submissive Behavior +
They are frightened to talk to outsiders and authorities since their trafficker(s) or exploiters closely monitor and control them. They appear withdrawn and neglected.
🪪 Lack of Official Identification +
They do not have their passports or other legal documents to allow them to travel. They do not have any legal identification such as a license, birth certificate, or Proof of Age card. An employer may have confiscated such documents.
💊 Substance Abuse +
They may show signs of drug use or addiction. They can be forced or coerced into drug use by their traffickers, or turn to substance abuse to help cope with their enslavement. Traffickers use drugs and alcohol to make victims vulnerable and tie them into slavery — this is particularly common in human trafficking and sexual exploitation.
🎒 Lack of Personal Belongings +
They may have few or no personal possessions. They seem to wear the same clothes every day or wear unsuitable clothing for their work. They do not have access to their belongings, such as their mobile phone.
🩹 Signs of Physical Abuse +
They may have bruises, scars, and other signs of physical or psychological abuse and torture. They may be "branded" with a tattoo indicating ownership. They may appear scared, intimidated, or frightened. They may demonstrate inappropriate affection, attachment, or dependence toward their abuser. Relationships may not seem right — for example, a young teenager appearing to be the boyfriend/girlfriend of a much older adult.
📋 Deceived by a False Job Offer +
Their actual job is different from the advertised job they accepted. They are deceived about working conditions and do not know the exact location of their employment. They do not have a contract, are paid less than the National Minimum Wage, or are not paid at all. They lack protective equipment, suitable clothing, or training to do their job safely.
🚗 Controlled Movement +
They are always dropped off at or collected from work very early in the morning or late at night. They seem unfamiliar with their neighborhood or workplace. There are controlled activities around the person's health and medical treatment. They are not free to meet up with others. Children are dropped off or picked up in private cars or taxis at unusual times and places.
🌐 Foreign, Unfamiliar with the Language +
They have recently arrived in the country and do not speak the local language — or they only know sex-related or labor-related words. Several female foreign nationals living at the same address could indicate sexual exploitation.
🔒 Isolation +
They are rarely allowed to travel on their own or interact with others. They don't have access to their belongings and are kept from their mobile phone. They are always accompanied or monitored. They don't know what is in their neighborhood. They could be linguistically isolated and unable to learn the local language. They live and work at the same residence and cannot leave on their own.

If You See It, Report It

Do not confront the individuals or cause a scene — this may lead to increased harm for the victims.

Instead, call the relevant reporting hotline or the police. If potential modern slavery is identified within a business context, refer to your internal policies and procedures for reporting. In an emergency, always call the police.

For more information, visit It's a Penalty.

Mandatory Reporting for Offenses Against Children: Some countries and states have strict reporting requirements for offenses against children. Please refer to your region's mandatory reporting obligations.

Lesson 4

Driving Forces of Modern Slavery

Root Causes and Vulnerability

Slavery results from social, economic, political, cultural, institutional, and environmental factors. The 2023 Vulnerability Model (Global Slavery Index 2023) highlights 23 risk variables across five significant dimensions:

Governance issues Lack of basic needs Inequality Disenfranchised groups Effects of conflict

A review of global data suggests that those most vulnerable to modern slavery are already victims of some form of social, economic, and political injustice. Poverty, social stigma and exclusion, discrimination based on race, gender, caste, and ethnicity, systemic marginalization, inadequate access to education and healthcare, food insecurity, and limited access to clean water all contribute to the vulnerability of a person or group to exploitation.

The Economics of Exploitation

In 2024, the International Labour Organization estimated that forced labour in the private economy generates approximately US$236 billion in illegal profits each year. Nearly US$99 billion of these profits come from commercial sexual exploitation, while the remainder is generated through forced labour in sectors such as industry, agriculture, domestic work, and services, where workers are coerced and denied their wages.

Source: International Labour Organization (2024), Profits and Poverty: The Economics of Forced Labour

Conflict and Climate Change

External factors such as armed conflict and climate change also drive modern slavery. The instability that war or a natural disaster brings to a region often displaces large communities, exposing them to extreme harm and exploitation — most often forced labor. Traffickers target displaced people such as migrants and refugees and prey on their desperation with the false promise of work. Vulnerable people are prone to making risky decisions for their family's survival, finding themselves in exploitative situations.

Slavery thrives in places where laws inadequately fail to criminalize — and therefore fail to prosecute — slave traders. Cultural practices also reinforce such acts, further perpetuating the generational cycle of exploitation and abuse.

The Dark Side of Supply Chains

Today's global supply chains create networks that span across millions of suppliers in multiple continents and countries, sourcing and supplying goods and services to consumers. Unfortunately, these extended and complex networks often lack transparency, creating potential opportunities for worker exploitation at multiple tiers in the supply chain, across various industries.

Industries especially vulnerable to exploitative conditions include:

⚙️ Electronics & high-tech
🚗 Steel & automobiles
🌾 Agriculture & seafood
⛏️ Mining & minerals
👕 Garments & textiles
🚢 Shipping & transportation

Lack of transparency and oversight in a business's operations and supply chains may harbor and promote modern slavery, such as forced and child labor.

Lesson 5

Legislation & Guidelines

The Role of Governments

Governments play a crucial role in developing and implementing laws, policies, and programs to criminalize all forms of modern slavery. In almost every corner of the globe, governments work in collaboration with the private sector, international organizations, and civil society. As a result, many countries have proposed and introduced domestic legislation to combat modern slavery risks, including mandatory modern slavery reporting and due diligence requirements.

Note: Most of these laws have been modelled on the recommendations set out in the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs) and the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises.

For Businesses

While companies may not overtly condone modern slavery, many meet only the bare minimum to comply — often out of necessity to fulfill a regulatory reporting requirement. By simply ticking boxes, companies turn a blind eye to the root causes and perpetuate the problem.

However, by increasing supply chain transparency, companies can identify, manage, and mitigate risks of harm hidden deep in their supply chains and turn the tide of exploitation.
